Abstract: The essay analyses the links between representation and power in balkanist discourse. It opens with the claim that, despite the influence of colonial discourse analysis on the study of balkanism, there is still a lack of research into the forms of imperial power that underlie Western representation of South-East Europe. The essay proceeds to use British travel writing on the region from 1850 to 19 14 as an exemplar of how such examination might be pursued. Focusing on such travellers as Edward Lear, William Miller and Florence Berger, the essay explores how literary representation so often supported the economic and diplomatic policies that Britain pursued in the Balkans during the period, including financial investment and the propping up of the Ottoman Empire in the face of Russian imperialism.
